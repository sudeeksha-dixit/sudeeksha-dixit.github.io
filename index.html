Product Management · Case Study Exercise

Reducing late deliveries on Swiggy

Analysed Swiggy's delivery experience as a user and product thinker. Identified that the core problem wasn't logistics — it was the silence. Designed a solution focused on communication and trust.

Type
PM Case Study Exercise
Deliverables
PRD + Wireframes
Tools
Notion · Google Sheets

The problem

As a regular Swiggy user, I've experienced late deliveries — and the frustrating part wasn't always the delay itself. It was not knowing why. The estimated time would sit static on screen while the delivery partner had clearly stopped moving, with no explanation.

I pulled public data from app store reviews and Reddit threads to validate this wasn't just my experience. The consistent pattern: users weren't only frustrated by lateness — they were frustrated by the silence.

"Most churn happens not because something went wrong — but because nobody said anything when it did."

Root cause analysis

I mapped the late delivery experience into three failure layers:

  • Logistics — restaurant prep time underestimation, traffic, partner availability
  • Communication — app doesn't update ETA in real time, no proactive notification sent
  • Recovery — support is reactive, no automatic gesture offered after a bad experience

I focused on the communication and recovery layers — these are product problems, faster to ship, and directly what users complained about.

Proposed solution — 3 features

  • Feature 1 (P0) — Proactive delay notification: When the system detects an order is 10+ minutes late, automatically send a plain-language push notification with the reason and revised ETA. "Your delivery partner is stuck in traffic near Koramangala. New ETA: 8:47 PM."
  • Feature 2 (P1) — Redesigned tracking screen: Add a status strip with delay reason + revised ETA in a yellow banner. No user action needed — just honesty, surfaced automatically.
  • Feature 3 (P2) — Automatic recovery offer: If an order arrives 15+ minutes late, automatically trigger a small coupon — without the user needing to complain. Turns a bad moment into a loyalty gesture.

What I learned

Communication is a product feature. Shipping better delay notifications is faster, cheaper, and immediately measurable — while the underlying logistics problem takes months to fix. Empathy mapping helped me realise users wanted to feel respected and informed, not just to receive faster food.

Product Management · PRD

ResearchOS — Internal AI research tool

A full PRD for a tool that standardises how teams use AI for research. Directly anchored in a real experience at ITWCP where I cut research cycle time by 40% — and then thought about what that system would look like properly built.

Type
Product Management · PRD
Deliverables
PRD · Personas · MoSCoW · Metrics
Tools
Notion · Excel · ChatGPT

Real-world anchor

At ITWCP, I automated parts of our market research workflow using GenAI and prompt engineering — cutting cycle time by 40%. But it was scrappy: prompts lived in my personal ChatGPT window, outputs weren't saved, and nothing was reusable by the team. ResearchOS is the version I would have built with more time.

The problem

  • Everyone uses different prompts — inconsistent output quality across the team
  • Research outputs live in personal chat windows — insights get lost, nothing is searchable
  • New joiners start from zero — no access to prompts the team has already refined

Personas

  • Priya (PM): Spends 30 mins per research task just figuring out how to prompt. Needs consistent, fast output so she can focus on decisions.
  • Rohan (Analyst): Rewrites the same prompts every week. Needs a library he can pull from so each week builds on the last.
  • Nina (New joiner): Uncertain whether her AI outputs match the team's quality. Needs guided templates from day one.

Must-have features (v1)

  • Prompt template library categorised by task type (competitor scan, interview synthesis, market sizing)
  • Run prompt → structured output (not raw chat window)
  • Save output with title, date, analyst name, and tags
  • Basic search by tag or keyword across saved outputs
  • Paste text or upload a document to analyse

Growth Marketing · Content Strategy

LinkedIn content engine — 30-day B2B strategy

Designed a full 30-day organic content strategy for Loopify, a fictional B2B SaaS targeting customer success teams. Built the framework, calendar, tracking system, and derived data-backed insights.

Type
Growth Marketing
Deliverables
Content calendar · Excel tracker · Insights
Tools
Excel · Notion · Google Analytics

The problem

Most B2B SaaS companies post on LinkedIn without a strategy — company news, product updates, occasional blog reposts. Flat engagement, no compounding effect, no inbound. I wanted to design what a proper organic LinkedIn engine looks like for an early-stage B2B SaaS with a small marketing function.

The framework — 4 post types

  • Insight: Counterintuitive take. Makes the target audience nod or disagree. Builds credibility.
  • Tactical: Framework or numbered list. Something they can use today. Drives saves.
  • Story: First-person honest moment. What went wrong, what I learned. Builds trust.
  • Proof: Mini outcome story. Outcome-first, no direct sell. Drives inbound profile visits.

3 posts per week, rotating through all 4 types so the feed never feels repetitive. No two "proof" posts in a row.

What the data showed

Tactical posts drove 3× more saves than story posts. Proof posts drove the most profile visits — outcome-first stories sent people to the page to find out what Loopify actually is.

Every post ending with "link in bio" or "DM me" saw ~30% lower impressions. LinkedIn suppresses outbound intent in the algorithm — the fix was giving all the value in the post and letting curious readers come to you.

Modelled results — 30 days

~4.2K
weekly impressions (from ~800)
5.1%
avg. engagement rate (from 1.5%)
9
inbound DMs / soft leads

What I learned

Consistency beats quality in month one. Three good posts per week beats one great post every two weeks. And organic LinkedIn is a slow channel — the data doesn't become meaningful until week 6–8. The trap is changing strategy before you have enough signal.

Growth Marketing · GTM Strategy

PipelineOS — AI feature GTM & demand generation

Built a complete zero-paid-ads GTM plan for a new AI forecasting feature at a fictional RevOps SaaS. Segmented the ICP, wrote targeted email sequences, designed sales collateral, and mapped an intent-driven content calendar.

Type
Growth Marketing
Deliverables
ICP map · Email sequences · Sales collateral · Content plan
Tools
Excel · Canva · Notion · Google Analytics

The problem

PipelineOS shipped a new AI forecasting feature with no GTM plan. Sales didn't know how to position it. Marketing hadn't touched it. The feature existed — and nobody outside the team knew. This is one of the most common ways good features die quietly at early-stage B2B SaaS companies.

ICP segmentation

I mapped 3 buyer segments in Excel before writing a single word of copy:

  • Primary — Head of RevOps (50–200 employees): Manual forecasting eating 6+ hours per week. Message: "Get your Monday back."
  • Secondary — VP of Sales (100–500 employees): Forecast accuracy too low to plan hiring. Message: "Stop guessing, start planning."
  • Stretch — Founder / COO (10–50 employees): No dedicated ops team. Message: "One tool, no analyst needed."

What I built

  • 9 targeted emails (3 per segment) — each under 150 words, one CTA, triggered by behaviour not just day count
  • One-pager: feature benefits + one proof point, designed in Canva
  • Battle card: 5 objections + 5 responses for sales reps who need to explain AI forecasting simply
  • Demo script: 10-minute flow with context-setting, walkthrough, and Q&A prompts
  • 8 intent-driven blog posts mapped to each ICP and tracked in Google Analytics

Modelled results — 6 weeks

24%
feature adoption (from 7% baseline)
~10%
email CTR to demo page
~12
demo requests from content alone

What I learned

GTM is a coordination problem, not a content problem. Features die quietly because sales, marketing, and product never agreed on the ICP before launch. Segmentation before writing changes every downstream piece — the angle, the proof point, the CTA. The battle card and demo script were the most immediately useful assets. Content takes weeks to rank. A battle card helps the sales team on Monday.
